Landmarks - General Info

    Famous and iconic landmark, that have mainly become tourist attraction sites today, encorporates China's 5000 years worth of history. These landmarks, whether naturally made or artificially composed, are treated as national treasures in China. Some popular Chinese landmarks includes Mount Everest, the Terracotta Army, the Forbidden City, and the Great Wall of China. Each structure of geographical feature orginates from different dynasties.

Yangtze River

The Yangtze River is not only the largest river in China, but also the largest in Asia. It is the third longest river in the world, and this river is approximately 6300 kilometers long, traveling through 9 provinces. The Yangtze River is acknowledged as the "Mother River" of China, and it had contributed significantly to the development of the nation. For example, the river was utilized by the people for irrigation, transportation, sanitation, and as a food source. The Yangtze River starts in Qinghai, and ends in the East China Sea, flowing from east to west. However, in modern times, the Yangtze River is unfortunately, suffering from industrial pollution and drainage.

The Terracotta Army

The Terracotta Army refers monument filled with 8000 life-sized clay soldiers, located in the Shaanxi province. Created in the Qin dynasty (221BC - 206BC), this army's purpose was to attend China's first emperor, Qin Shi Huang, after his death. He believed that by creating this army of clay soldiers and being buried with them, he would experience the same luxery of protection and imperial status in the afterlife. Qin Shi Huang forced hundreds of thousands of civilians to construct the Terracotta Army. Interestingly, each clay soldier was created delicately, as they all had distinct features, not one is identical.

The Great Wall of China

One of the most notable landmarks of China, and also the symbol of China. The Great Wall of China is located in Beijing, and was built in the Qin Dynasty, starting in 221 BC. Thus, the Great Wall of China is about 2700 years old today. Emperor Qin Shi Huang ordered hundreds of thousands of soldiers and convicts to build the wall. The construction environment was so lethal it had killed nearly 400000 people during its process. It was built with the military intentions to stop the invasion of Mongolians. This construction continued until the Ming Dynasty, becoming a total of 13000 miles in length. More than a million people, total, worked on constructing the Great Wall.

Mount Everest

Mount Everest is known as the highest point on Earth, possessing a height of 29035 feet. It is the tallest mountain of the Himilayan mountain range, located between Nepal and Tibet. Mount Everest is a dangerous tourist place, though some visit purposely for such expedition. Oxygen is extremely limited at the summit of the mountain and the temperature plummets to a lethal extent due to the mountain's high altitude. The apparant side effects of traveling to the "death zone" of Mount Everest, the area 26000 feet in height, include brain swelling and altitude sickness.

The Forbidden City

The Forbidden City is the name of China's imperial palace, home to 24 emperors. The Forbidden City is located at the center of Beijing, taking 14 years and more than one million laborers to complete its construction. First built in 1406, it became China's official imperial palace from 1420 to 1912, totaling to 492 years. The Forbidden City has more than 90 palace quarters and courtyards, and about 8730 buildings. It has a phenomenal area of 7.75 million square feet, being the world's largest imperial palace. Surrounding the palace is the protection of a 10 meter high wall.

The Summer Palace

The Summer Palace is an imperial garden located in northwest Beijing, built in 1750 as commanded by Emperor Qianglong, the fourth emperor of the Qing Dynasty. It was called the Garden of Clear Ripples by the emperor, who initiated the project as a gift for his mother's birthday. The Summer Palace is well preserved and the largest imperial garden in the world. Although originally created for the purpose of providing the imperial palace with a water supply, it became a place for emperors to rest and for their passtime.

FUN FACT: Some of the workers who died building the Great Wall of China were buried inside of the wall.
