Chinese Food - General Info

    The variety of dishes and their styles all originate from the 8 main culinary cuisines comprised of Anhui, Cantonese, Fujian, Hunan, Jiangsu, Shandong, Sichuan, and Zhejiang. Although previously the cuisines that were most acknowledged were Chuan, Lu, Yue, and Huaiyang, called the Four Great Cuisines, in modern times, the previously mentioned 8 cuisines are approved of instead. Each region has their own cultural preference in flavors and prime ingredients for their dishes.

Anhui

Anhui cuisine, otherwise also known as Hui, is one of the less known Chinese cuisine. The most prominent flavors of Hui dishes are light, salty, and fresh. Anhui was a poor province as compared to the other provinces of China, and thus, the ingredients for their dishes were most certainly from the wilderness. This consisted of the staple food of rice, various fungi, animals such as small shrimp, frogs, and etc. Examples of Anhui dishes:

  • Stewed Bamboo Shoots
  • Huangshan Stewed Pigeon
  • Salted Mandarin Fish

Cantonese

Cantonese styled dishes are the most popular in China, the flavor being slightly sweet, though overall mild. Cantonese cuisine first cultivated in the Guangdong province, and they are most notable for rice and seafood dishes. There is limited flavorings like spice that is used, as Cantonese cuisine's notion towards maintaining the ingredient's original flavors. The preperation methods used for Cantonese dishes are mostly steaming and stir frying, which is least likely to alter the original taste of the ingredients within the food. Some popular Cantonese food include:

  • Chinese Steamed Eggs
  • Dim Sum
  • White Cut Chicken

Fujian

For Fujian cuisine dishes, places in Fujian were typically poorer than other prosperous cities such as Beijing or Shanghai. Thus, the food that originates from Fujian were generally made from ingredients that were wild. For example, wild herbs, varying species of mushrooms,and seafood are used as prime ingredients, thus creating nutritious meals. The flavor that dominates most Fujian foods is a mild sweet and sour taste. The dish that is most commonly served in Fujian, as a tradition, is soup. The Fujian province is known for soup and seafood dishes.

  • Boiled Sea Clam With Chicken Soup
  • Oyster Omelette

Hunan

Although Sichuan cuisine is known for being spicy, Hunan cuisine is even more intensely spicy, less numbing, and more sour. Hunan dishes includes ingredients such as a variety of chilli peppers, which gives most Hunan dishes its spicy taste, and also includes citrus fruits, giving Hunan dishes its sour taste. Methods of cooking preferred in the Hunan province includes stir-frying, sauteing, steaming, and smoking.

  • Hunan Orange Chicken
  • Spare Ribs Steamed in Bamboo

Jiangsu

The most appealing property of Jiangsu cuisine is their notable elegant and refined appearance for each dish served. Originating from the Qin dynasty, these dishes are mostly seafood based. The standardized cooking methods used for Jiangsu dishes are braising, stewing, and simmering. Similarly to Cantonese cuisine, Jiangsu cuisine also aim to presere the orginal flavor of the dish's ingredients.

  • Brine-Boiled Duck
  • Sweet and Sour Mandarin Fish

Shandong

With Shandong province's long coast, this made it so that most of their dishes used seafood, as it was a local ingredient. Out of all the cuisines, Shandong delivers the most original cuisine, as the Shandong province was the oldest province to have resided. They are known for their vegetable and seafood dishes and their salty and crunchy flavors.

  • Sweet and Sour Carp
  • Braised Sea Cucumber with Scallion
  • Cuttlefish Roe Soup

Sichuan

Sichuan is famous for their numbing spicy dishes, also using Sichuan pepper as their main spicy ingredient. There are also numerous other commonly used seasonings in Sichuan, such as garlic, black pepper, bean chilli paste, and other chilli peppers. Each dish has their own style of cooking, rather than mainly 2 or 3, like the other cuisines. Famous dishes originating from Sichuan includes:

  • Mapo Tofu
  • Kung Pao Chicken
  • Sichuan Hot Pot

Zhejiang

Known for the light, tender, and seafood-based flavors for their dishes, and most importantly, the ingredient used are strictly seasonal and fresh. A particular wine called Shaoxing yellow wine is used as part of the cooking process, which formulates one of Zhejiang's cooking style. Their cooking methods vary greatly for each dish, however, for seafood, it is common for Zhejiang dishes to be soaked in salty brine and then cooked.

  • Fried Shrimp in Longjing Tea
  • West Lake Fish in Vinegar Gravy

FUN FACT: It is considered extremely impolite to leave chopsticks in a bowl of rice vertically up, as it resembles the traditional funeral procedure.
